# COMPLIANCE — Australian advertising rules (read before writing any copy)

This is practical guidance distilled from public AHPRA material, not legal advice. The regulator does not pre-approve advertising, so the clinic should confirm final copy with its indemnity insurer or a legal adviser. Apply these rules to every page, including the coming-soon page, because a coming-soon page still advertises a regulated health service.

## Why this applies

Chiropractic is a registered profession under the National Law, so AHPRA's advertising rules apply directly. Exercise physiology is not AHPRA-registered (it is accredited through ESSA), but the Australian Consumer Law still prohibits misleading or deceptive claims, and since this is one brand advertising chiropractic alongside it, the safe approach is to hold the whole site to the AHPRA standard. Dry needling is a technique and should be described factually, not as a cure.

## Hard blocklist (never do these)

1. **No testimonials or reviews that reference clinical care or outcomes.** This is an absolute prohibition under section 133 of the National Law. It covers your own site and any reviews you republish. Do not build a testimonials section. Do not invent patient stories. Do not quote outcomes.
2. **No outcome or cure language.** Banned words and phrases: pain-free, cured, cure, heal you, fix, fixed, guaranteed, instant, fast results, miracle. Outcomes vary between people and cannot be promised.
3. **No comparative or superlative claims.** No "best", "number one", "leading", "better than other clinics".
4. **No encouraging unnecessary or indiscriminate use.** Do not imply that regular or preventative visits are needed to stay healthy. Do not run pressure offers or countdown discounts that push unnecessary care.
5. **No "specialist" language.** Chiropractic has no recognised specialties under the scheme. Do not write "specialist in" anything. If describing a focus, use plain factual phrasing such as "works with runners and post-operative clients".
6. **"Dr" must be clarified.** If a practitioner uses "Dr", state the profession immediately, for example "Dr [Name], Chiropractor", so no one reads it as a medical doctor.
7. **Patient images and video need written consent.** Any future real photo or video of an identifiable patient requires explicit written consent specifying the use. Until then, do not depict identifiable patients.

## What is allowed (use these freely)

- Factual service descriptions: what each service is, in plain terms.
- Practical session facts: first-visit length, what an assessment involves, what to bring.
- Qualifications and registration: that a practitioner is a registered chiropractor or an accredited exercise physiologist.
- Location, hours, contact, parking, accessibility.
- Educational content about movement and training, written generally, not as a promised result.
- Reviews or feedback that mention only experience, not clinical outcomes: clean space, friendly staff, easy booking, good location. (Still not required, and not for launch.)

## Copy checklist (run on every block before shipping)

1. Does it state or imply a guaranteed or specific clinical outcome? If yes, rewrite.
2. Does it contain any banned word from the list above? If yes, remove.
3. Is it a testimonial or does it reference what treatment did for someone? If yes, delete.
4. Does it compare the clinic to others or use a superlative? If yes, remove.
5. Does it push regular or preventative visits as necessary? If yes, reframe to factual.
6. If "Dr" appears, is the profession stated right next to it? If no, fix.
7. Does it depict an identifiable patient without documented consent? If yes, replace with a placeholder.
8. Is every claim factual and able to be substantiated? If unsure, soften to plain description.

## Safe framing for this brand

Because the Zone Two idea is about building a base and returning to movement, you can be aspirational without promising outcomes. Talk about capability, the work involved, and getting back to what people love to do. Describe what a session is, not what it will achieve. That keeps the copy both on-brand and compliant.
